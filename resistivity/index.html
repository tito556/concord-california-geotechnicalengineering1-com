Electrical Resistivity Surveys and Vertical Electrical Sounding (VES) in Concord, California

Together, we solve the challenges of tomorrow.

LEARN MORE →

ASTM D6431 and ASTM D7128 set the standard for surface geophysics, and in Concord, California, these methods solve problems that conventional drilling alone cannot. The city sits within the Suisun Bay watershed, where alluvial fan deposits from Mount Diablo meet estuarine clays of the Ygnacio Valley. That transition creates a subsurface puzzle: coarse channel gravels pinch out against expansive clay lenses over short lateral distances. Vertical Electrical Sounding, or VES, maps these contrasts by injecting a known current through four electrodes and measuring the resulting potential difference. The apparent resistivity values are then inverted to produce a layered geoelectric model, giving engineers a continuous profile of strata changes without the gaps inherent in spaced borings. For projects near the Concord Naval Weapons Station redevelopment zone, where fill history is irregular, this non-invasive approach provides critical baseline data for foundation depth decisions and earthwork classification. We often pair resistivity lines with seismic refraction to cross-validate the top-of-bedrock pick in areas where the Great Valley Sequence shales weather to a conductive clay rind that can mask the true refusal depth.

In Concord's Ygnacio Valley, a VES sounding can distinguish a clean gravel aquifer from a clay-bound gravel in under an hour, without a single drill rig mobilization.

How we work

A resistivity survey run through the older central Concord neighborhoods, east of Todos Santos Plaza, typically reveals a three-layer model: a 1.5 to 3 meter thick dry sandy silt cap of 150–400 ohm-m, underlain by a conductive clay unit below 25 ohm-m, with a resistive basement rising toward the Lime Ridge Open Space. Contrast that with a line out near the Buchanan Fields area, west of I-680, where the Quaternary alluvium is thicker and the intermediate conductor often splits into two distinct aquitard-aquifer pairs. That second scenario demands a longer Wenner or Schlumberger spread to reach the bedrock asymptote. Our field team uses a Syscal Pro 96-channel resistivity meter with stainless steel stakes, recording multiple array geometries in a single setup. The data is processed in RES2DINV and EarthImager 2D, with topographic correction applied from survey-grade GPS. For Concord projects requiring aggregate prospecting or groundwater exploration, we run dipole-dipole and pole-dipole arrays to sharpen lateral resolution on gravel channels that follow the paleo-drainages of Walnut Creek. Each profile is calibrated against at least one existing boring log when available, tying the geoelectric boundaries to USCS soil classifications per ASTM D2487.

Local ground factors

On the west side of Concord, particularly in the area bounded by Willow Pass Road and Port Chicago Highway, we frequently encounter a thin resistive cap that masks a deep, saturated clay layer with resistivity below 10 ohm-m. When that clay is misinterpreted as bedrock in a quick-look pseudosection, the foundation design can end up short, bearing on a compressible stratum instead of the competent Franciscan bedrock. The consequence shows up later as differential settlement, especially in slab-on-grade warehouses where live loads are uneven. Another local pitfall is the seasonal shift in near-surface resistivity: the clay-rich topsoil in Concord can swing from 80 ohm-m in September to under 20 ohm-m after a wet January, which changes the current penetration efficiency. We run resistivity lines in both wet and dry seasons when a project spans a full year, or we normalize the data using on-site soil box measurements from shallow test pit samples. That extra step keeps the inverted sections consistent and defensible during plan review with the Contra Costa County Building Department.

Other technical services

01

1D Vertical Electrical Sounding (VES)

A single-point Schlumberger or Wenner sounding that expands electrode spacing logarithmically to build a layered resistivity-depth model. Ideal for determining depth to bedrock, identifying the freshwater-saline interface near the Suisun Bay margin, or mapping the thickness of compressible Bay Mud equivalents in the Ygnacio Valley sub-basin.

02

2D Electrical Resistivity Tomography (ERT)

A multi-electrode profile collected with 48 or 96 channels, producing a continuous cross-section of subsurface resistivity. Used for locating abandoned underground storage tanks on redevelopment parcels, tracing paleochannel gravels for aggregate resource evaluation, and delineating the lateral extent of fill materials at former industrial sites along the Highway 4 corridor.

Common questions

How deep can a VES survey in Concord really see?

With a standard 200-meter maximum current electrode spread, we typically resolve strata down to 50 or 60 meters in Concord's alluvial environment. The actual depth of investigation depends on the resistivity contrast and the ambient noise level. In quiet areas like Lime Ridge Open Space, the signal penetrates deeper; along busy arterials like Monument Boulevard with overhead power lines, we sometimes lose the deep signal and cap the inversion at 30 to 35 meters.

What's the cost range for an electrical resistivity survey in Concord?

For a typical Concord site, a single VES sounding runs between US$590 and US$850. A full 2D resistivity tomography profile of 200 to 400 linear meters with 48 electrodes generally falls in the US$800 to US$1,000 range, depending on access and the need for traffic control.

Do you need to drill boreholes to calibrate the resistivity data?

The reference range for this service in Concord California is US$590 - US$1.000. The final price depends on the project scope and volume.

How long does a resistivity survey take on a Concord job site?

A single VES sounding with a two-person crew takes about 45 to 90 minutes on site, including setup and takedown. A 400-meter 2D ERT line with 96 electrodes typically requires a half-day of field work. Data processing and interpretation add another two to three business days before the draft report is ready for review.
