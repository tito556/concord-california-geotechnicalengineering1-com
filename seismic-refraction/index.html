Concord sits at just 75 feet above sea level, yet portions of the East Bay city overlie deep alluvial basins that amplify seismic waves in ways a standard boring log will never reveal. Our team deploys seismic tomography—both P-wave refraction and S-wave reflection—to map shear-wave velocity profiles and bedrock geometry across the 122-square-kilometer city footprint. For the five-story mixed-use structures going up near Todos Santos Plaza or the warehouse expansions in the North Concord industrial corridor, the difference between a generic site class D assumption and a measured Vs30 of 260 m/s can shift the design base shear by thirty percent. We bridge that gap with MASW surveys when the site demands a non-invasive surface-wave complement, and we correlate tomographic cross-sections with CPT soundings to anchor velocity models to measured tip resistance and pore-pressure data in the deeper basin fill.

A 2D refraction tomogram will reveal a buried paleochannel that twenty borings on a 50-foot grid can miss entirely—and in Concord’s alluvial setting, that channel is where the differential settlement lives.

How we work

What we often observe in Concord is a sharp velocity contrast at 15 to 25 feet depth, where Holocene alluvium of the Central Valley margin transitions into the underlying Franciscan Complex bedrock. Seismic refraction tomography captures that interface with a resolution a resistivity line or a single downhole shot simply cannot match. Field crews lay out 24- to 48-channel geophone spreads with 5-foot spacing, using a 16-pound sledge or an accelerated weight drop depending on the target depth, and we invert travel-time picks with iterative damped least-squares algorithms to produce 2D P-wave tomograms. When the project requires shear-wave velocities for site classification under ASCE 7-22 Chapter 20, we switch to SH-wave reflection profiling or integrate seismic refraction with MASW to build a continuous Vs30 map across the parcel. Every profile we deliver includes RMS error residuals and ray-coverage density plots so the geotechnical reviewer can assess model reliability without chasing raw field files. On sites where soft clay lenses are suspected, we also recommend in-situ permeability testing to evaluate consolidation timeframes that affect the foundation schedule.

Local ground factors

A four-story medical office building on Clayton Road was designed assuming Site Class D based on regional maps and two borings that terminated at 50 feet in stiff clay. The structural engineer had locked the lateral system before anyone looked deeper. When the owner authorized a refraction-MASW combined survey, the tomogram showed a low-velocity trough—Vs below 500 ft/s down to 65 feet—that pushed the site into Site Class E territory. The resulting base-shear increase forced a lateral redesign that added six weeks to the permit timeline and a mid-five-figure change order. The lesson we keep seeing in Concord: basin-edge velocity gradients are too steep for interpolation. Seismic tomography gives you a continuous cross-section, not a point estimate, and when the next rupture on the Concord-Green Valley Fault occurs, the difference between those two levels of knowledge will be measured in drift ratios and repair costs.

Common questions

How much does a seismic refraction survey cost for a typical commercial lot in Concord?

For a standard commercial parcel of one to two acres in Concord, a combined refraction-plus-MASW survey with a 24-channel array and weight-drop source typically runs between US$2,390 and US$5,470. The range depends on line length, number of spreads, and whether SH-wave reflection is added for Vs30 profiling. We provide a fixed-price proposal after reviewing the site plan and depth requirements.

What depth can seismic tomography reach in the Concord basin?

With a 240-foot geophone spread and an accelerated weight drop, P-wave refraction reliably reaches 80 to 100 feet in Concord’s alluvial soils. If the target is deeper—say 150 feet for a parking structure with two basement levels—we extend the spread length or use a larger energy source. The limiting factor is usually the velocity contrast at the top of the Franciscan bedrock, which creates a strong refractor that masks deeper layers.

Can seismic tomography replace borings for site classification?

Seismic tomography provides continuous velocity data that borings alone cannot, but it does not replace them. ASCE 7-22 allows Vs30 from geophysical methods for site classification, yet borings are still needed for soil type, strength, and groundwater confirmation. We recommend pairing at least one boring with the tomography lines so the velocity model can be calibrated to known lithology and the geotechnical report satisfies both the building official and the structural reviewer.

How close to the Concord-Green Valley Fault can you run a reflection line?

We routinely position SH-wave reflection lines within 200 feet of mapped fault traces in Concord, using high-fold acquisition and careful static corrections to image near-vertical offsets. The key is orienting the line perpendicular to the fault strike and processing with dip-moveout algorithms that preserve steep reflectors. The result is a subsurface image that helps the project geologist confirm whether Holocene deformation extends into the building footprint.
